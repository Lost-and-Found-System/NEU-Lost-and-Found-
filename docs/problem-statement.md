## Problem Statement
New Era University (NEU) students frequently lose personal items on campus — school IDs, keys, wallets, electronics, and clothing. When a fellow student finds one of these items, that knowledge (where it was found, what it looks like, who found it) is currently:

- Shared informally through Facebook group posts that disappear in feeds  
- Posted on physical bulletin boards that are rarely checked  
- Passed through word-of-mouth chains that break quickly  
- Reported to the guard booth with no digital record  

The result is that knowledge about found items is fragmented, siloed, and hard to find. Items often sit in the guard booth for weeks unclaimed because no one knows they are there. Students replace lost IDs and wallets unnecessarily. The knowledge exists — it is just trapped in the wrong format, in the wrong place.

This is a classic knowledge management problem: tacit knowledge (“I know where your wallet is”) that needs to be converted into explicit, searchable, persistent knowledge (“there is a record of your wallet in a database”).  

Without a structured KM system, the university community suffers from:  
- **Knowledge loss**: Items are not properly recorded.  
- **Retrieval inefficiency**: Owners cannot easily locate their belongings.  
- **Siloed information**: Records are scattered across individuals instead of a shared repository.  

The consequences include wasted time, frustration among students and staff, and reduced trust in the institution’s ability to manage personal property. A dedicated Lost and Found KM system is needed to capture, store, and retrieve this knowledge effectively.
